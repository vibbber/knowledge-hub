What We Forget Becomes Who We Are

How Forgotten Experiences Shape Character, Courage, and Mood

Steiner's Insight

"We are essentially made up of what we have forgotten. Seen concretely, what are human beings other than how they are joyful or courageous, and so forth?"

What This Means

When we memorize something, we can recall and repeat it. But when we "forget" something—when it slips from conscious recall—it doesn't disappear. It sinks into deeper layers of the soul, where it becomes something else entirely:

  • Specific memories become moods
  • Stories of heroes become courage or cowardice
  • Early experiences become fears or confidence
  • What we learned becomes how we face life

Why Early Childhood Matters Most

We can only consciously remember back to a certain point in childhood. But before that moment, we had an "endless number of impressions." These are completely forgotten—yet they are the most formative.

"Much of the total attitude of adult souls depends, more than people usually suppose, on those early experiences."

Implications for Parents and Educators

  • The atmosphere matters more than the content. Children won't remember specific events, but they'll become the mood of their childhood.
  • Heroes and great lives shape character. Even when stories are forgotten, they leave behind courage, determination, or compassion.
  • Don't worry about immediate understanding. What's learned now will be understood later—but it's working on the soul from the start.
  • Create experiences worth forgetting. The best experiences are those that will sink into the soul and become character traits.
  • Be intentional about what surrounds the child. The presence of love, joy, and meaning becomes the child's fundamental orientation to life.

Source: Rudolf Steiner, "Interests, Talent, and Education" (Berlin, 1911)
