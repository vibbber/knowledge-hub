Color Environment for Child Temperament

Using Complementary Colors to Balance Excitable and Lethargic Children

How It Works

When children are surrounded by a color, their eyes and nervous system automatically generate the complementary color internally. This internal color creation is what affects the child's temperament—not the external color itself.

You can verify this yourself: stare at a red surface for 30 seconds, then quickly look at a white surface. You'll see green. This is the complementary color your system creates.

EXCITABLE CHILD

Surround with: Red / Reddish-Yellow

Signs: Nervous, overstimulated, restless, emotionally reactive, difficulty settling
External: RED → Internal: GREEN
Green has a calming effect on the organs
LETHARGIC CHILD

Surround with: Blue / Bluish-Green

Signs: Low energy, passive, slow to engage, unmotivated, sluggish responses
External: BLUE → Internal: ORANGE
Orange has an energizing effect on the organs

Where to Apply Colors

  • Room walls and curtains — The dominant colors in the child's bedroom or playroom
  • Clothing — Especially everyday clothes and pajamas
  • Bedding — Sheets, blankets, pillows
  • Play area — Rugs, mats, and play furniture
  • Objects in constant view — Art on walls, lamps, accessories

Complementary Color Pairs

External Color Creates Internally Effect
Red Green Calming
Orange Blue Cooling
Yellow Violet Deepening
Blue Orange Energizing
Violet Yellow Brightening

Common Mistake

Intuition often leads us to do the opposite—giving a calm, lethargic child soothing blue, or surrounding an excitable child with calming green. This actually reinforces the existing tendency instead of balancing it. Remember: the external color produces the opposite effect internally.

Source: Rudolf Steiner, "The Education of the Child in the Light of Spiritual Science" (1907)
