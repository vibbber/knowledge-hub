Teaching Writing from Pictures

From "The Kingdom of Childhood" by Rudolf Steiner (1924), Lecture 2

Core Principle

Never teach letters as abstract symbols. Children ages 7-10 are beings of imagination, not intellect. Letters emerged historically from pictures - let children recapitulate that journey. Writing should come before reading (the whole body is engaged in writing; only the head is engaged in reading).

Don't Do This

Show the letter "M" and say "This is M, it makes the mmm sound."

The child has no relationship to this abstract symbol. It's like showing a stranger's portrait and expecting love.

Do This Instead

Have children paint a mouth, say "mmm," then discover how the lips make the M shape.

The letter grows from something real and embodied.

The Method: 5 Steps

1 Start with Painting-Drawing

Have children paint or draw pictures of real things. Not abstract shapes - actual objects they know.

Use colors freely. The goal is expression, not precision.

2 Connect the Image to Its Sound

Once they've drawn the object, have them say the word. Then isolate the initial sound.

Example: Teaching "M"

"Paint a mouth with red color. Now say 'Mouth' slowly... what sound does it begin with? Mmmm. Look at your lips in a mirror when you say it. See how they come together at the top?"

3 Transform the Picture into the Letter

Guide children to see how the letter form emerges from the picture.

Consonant Examples
  • M - From the two peaks of the upper lip (mouth)
  • F - From the shape of a fish with fins
  • S - From a snake's winding path
  • W - From waves on water
  • B - From a bear's round body
Vowel Examples (from gestures)
  • A - Arms raised in wonder ("Ah!") - forms the A shape
  • O - Arms embracing with love - forms the O
  • E - Pointing at something ("Eh?") - angular like E
4 Practice Writing (Not Reading)

Once children understand letters as transformed pictures, practice writing them. The whole body is engaged - fingers, arm, posture.

"In writing... the whole human being is active. The fingers take part, the body is positioned, the whole person is engaged. In reading only the head is occupied."
5 Reading Comes Last

Only after children can write do you transition to reading. Reading is abstraction from writing, which is abstraction from pictures, which come from life.

Sequence: Life → Picture → Writing → Reading

Be Inventive!
You don't need to follow historical etymology. Invent your own picture-to-letter transformations. What matters is that the child experiences the letter as meaningful, not arbitrary. Each teacher can find their own creative path.

Why This Matters

  • Children 7-10 live in imagination, not intellect - this method honors that
  • The child's whole being is engaged, not just cognitive processing
  • Learning to write too early with abstract methods can hinder later spiritual development
  • The "magical signs" of letters feel demonic to children without this foundation
